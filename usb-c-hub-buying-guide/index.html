How to choose the right USB-C hub

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best USB‑C hub for you depends entirely on the ports your devices need, whether you require external display support, and how much power delivery you demand from your laptop. Start by listing your must‑have connections (USB‑A, HDMI, Ethernet, SD card) and your monitor setup, then choose a reputable brand that matches those requirements within your budget.

What ports do you actually need?

Think about the devices you plug in regularly. A typical USB‑C hub offers a mix of USB‑A ports (for older peripherals), an HDMI or DisplayPort output for an external monitor, a 3.5 mm audio jack, and often an Ethernet port for stable wired internet. Some include an SD or microSD card slot – essential if you work with camera files. Compact “dongle” hubs trade port count for portability: you might get only two USB‑A ports and a single HDMI. Full‑size desktop hubs add more ports, dedicated power passthrough, and sometimes a built‑in cable. The trade‑off is size and cost. Don’t overbuy – if you almost never use Ethernet or SD cards, a smaller hub will save space and money.

Do you need 4K or 5K display support?

Not all USB‑C hubs handle high‑resolution displays equally. Many support a single 4K display at 60 Hz, which is plenty for productivity and video streaming. Some hubs, especially those using DisplayLink technology, can drive dual external monitors – but this requires installing driver software and may not work seamlessly with every operating system. If you need 5K or 6K resolutions (like with Apple’s Pro Display XDR), look for hubs that explicitly list support for those resolutions. In general, Thunderbolt docks handle higher bandwidth displays better, but a good USB‑C hub can still drive a single 4K monitor without issues. Check your laptop’s video output capabilities too – some USB‑C ports may already support direct display output, while others rely on the hub’s chipset.

How important is power delivery?

Power delivery (PD) passthrough is one of the most critical features of a USB‑C hub. It lets you charge your laptop through the hub while using other ports. Hubs vary widely in the wattage they pass through: some deliver 60–85 W (enough for most ultrabooks), while others cap at 20–30 W (fine for tablets or phones but will slowly drain a 15‑inch laptop under heavy use). If you plan to run demanding tasks like video editing or gaming while connected to the hub, make sure the hub’s PD passthrough meets or exceeds your laptop’s original charger wattage. Also check that the hub itself doesn’t consume a significant portion of that power – some cheap hubs siphon off 5–10 W, leaving less for your device. A reputable model from a brand like Anker or CalDigit will specify the effective passthrough wattage clearly.

Should you choose a USB‑C hub or a Thunderbolt dock?

This is a common confusion point. A standard USB‑C hub uses the USB‑C connector and supports USB 3.2 or USB4 speeds (typically 10 Gbps to 40 Gbps on USB4). A Thunderbolt dock uses the same physical connector but runs on the Thunderbolt protocol (3 or 4) and guarantees higher bandwidth (40 Gbps), native dual‑display support, and daisy‑chaining of Thunderbolt devices. For most people – those connecting a mouse, keyboard, one external monitor, and maybe an Ethernet cable – a USB‑C hub is perfectly adequate and much cheaper (budget‑friendly). Thunderbolt docks are necessary if you need to run multiple high‑resolution displays, connect fast external GPUs, or transfer massive files between Thunderbolt SSDs. The price difference can be significant (a few hundred dollars vs. a more affordable hub), so choose based on your workflow, not just specs.

What data speed do you actually need?

USB‑C hubs advertise speeds like “5 Gbps”, “10 Gbps”, or “40 Gbps”. For everyday tasks – backing up photos, editing documents, streaming video – 5 Gbps (USB 3.2 Gen 1) is fine. If you regularly move large files, such as 4K video projects or large databases, a hub with 10 Gbps (USB 3.2 Gen 2) will halve transfer times. USB4 or Thunderbolt hubs push up to 40 Gbps, but the cost is higher and you need compatible devices to see that speed. Important nuance: many hubs share bandwidth among all ports. Plugging in a fast external SSD and an external display at the same time can reduce performance on both. Check reviews that test simultaneous usage – a well‑engineered hub from a brand like Belkin or Satechi handles this gracefully, while bargain models may drop speeds significantly. If you often use multiple high‑speed peripherals, consider a powered (external power adapter) hub that doesn’t rely on the laptop’s limited power budget.

How does size and portability affect everyday use?

The physical form of a USB‑C hub matters more than you might think. Stick‑style hubs (which plug directly into the laptop) are super portable but block adjacent ports, and they can strain the laptop’s port with heavy cables dangling off them. Cable‑connected hubs (a short cable from the hub to the laptop) are more flexible and put less stress on the port, but you have another cable to manage. Desktop hubs, often with a detachable cable and an external power brick, sit on your desk and offer better ventilation and more ports. The trade‑off: they’re not travel‑friendly. If you’re a road warrior, a compact cable‑connected hub from Anker or Ugreen will serve you well. If your laptop lives on a desk, a desktop hub like those from CalDigit provides a more solid, always‑ready setup. Also consider cable length – a short 6‑inch cable works for tight spaces, while a 12‑inch cable gives you more placement freedom.

Frequently asked questions

Can a USB‑C hub charge my laptop?

Yes, if the hub supports Power Delivery passthrough and you connect it to a USB‑C power adapter. Check that the hub’s passthrough wattage is equal to or higher than your laptop’s original charger. Most ultrabooks charge fine with 60 W passthrough, but larger laptops may need 85 W or more.

Will a USB‑C hub work with my USB‑C monitor?

It depends on the hub and monitor. Some hubs have a dedicated USB‑C port that supports video output (often labeled “DisplayPort Alt Mode”), which can drive a USB‑C monitor. Others only pass through data or power. If your monitor connects via USB‑C, look for a hub that explicitly lists video support over USB‑C.

Does a USB‑C hub support multiple monitors?

Most standard USB‑C hubs support only a single external display unless they use DisplayLink technology, which can add dual- or triple‑monitor support through software. Thunderbolt docks often natively support two or more displays. Check the hub’s specifications for the exact number and resolution of supported monitors.

What is the difference between a USB‑C hub and a Thunderbolt dock?

A USB‑C hub uses the USB protocol and typically offers lower bandwidth (5–40 Gbps), fewer display options, and no daisy‑chaining. A Thunderbolt dock uses the Thunderbolt protocol, guaranteeing 40 Gbps bandwidth, native multi‑display support, and the ability to chain devices. Thunderbolt docks are generally more expensive and overkill for basic peripherals.

How do I know if a hub is compatible with my device?

First, check your device’s USB‑C port capabilities – does it support DisplayPort Alt Mode, Power Delivery, and USB 3.2? Most modern laptops and tablets do, but some budget tablets or phones may lack video output or high‑power PD. Then read the hub’s compatibility list (many mention Windows, macOS, Chrome OS, and iPadOS). If your device uses Thunderbolt, it will also work with USB‑C hubs, but at USB speeds.

Do I need a powered hub for external drives?

If you regularly connect power‑hungry external hard drives (especially 3.5‑inch desktop drives or multiple SSDs), a powered hub with its own power adapter is strongly recommended. Bus‑powered “portable” hubs may not supply enough current to spin up multiple drives, causing them to disconnect randomly. A powered hub from a brand like CalDigit or Anker provides stable power and avoids data loss.

In shortThe right USB‑C hub is the one that matches your actual workflow, not the one with the most ports or the highest number on the box. Prioritise the ports you truly need, check that power delivery and display support align with your laptop, and choose a reputable brand with solid reviews on simultaneous usage. A well‑chosen hub will disappear into your setup – a bad one will be a constant source of frustration. Take your time to match the hub to your habits, and you’ll be set for years.
